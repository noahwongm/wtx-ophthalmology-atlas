    How the Composite Need Score works

    Each county's need score (0–100) is a weighted average of six standardized sub-scores: poverty rate, uninsured rate, median age, diagnosed-diabetes prevalence, HRSA primary-care shortage status, and drive time to an ophthalmologist. The default is equal 1:1 weighting, the transparent standard used by the CDC Social Vulnerability Index. Use the Composite Weighting panel to reweight the indicators, for example to give the two access factors (drive time, HPSA) more emphasis than the four socioeconomic factors, or to mirror HRSA's provider-weighted underservice logic. A county's score is only displayed once at least four of its six indicators are verified, so nothing is estimated from missing values. For how this weighting compares to the CDC Social Vulnerability Index and HRSA's Index of Medical Underservice, and why equal weighting is the default, see the companion methods note, Composite Need Score: How It Compares to Established Indices.
    Data status & sourcing. This atlas reports only verified, sourced figures. Metrics still marked pending verification are being confirmed one county at a time from primary sources and are shown with a hatched fill rather than a placeholder number. Sources by indicator: poverty — U.S. Census Bureau ACS 2020–2024 5-year estimates (table S1701); median age — ACS 2020–2024 5-year estimates (table B01002); under-65 uninsured — U.S. Census Bureau Small Area Health Insurance Estimates (SAHIE) 2023, all incomes; diabetes prevalence — CDC PLACES; primary-care shortage status — HRSA Health Professional Shortage Area designations; ophthalmology access — estimated driving time from each county's population center to the nearest ophthalmologist (surgical eye MD), using verified practice locations from Google Places and straight-line distance scaled by a 1.2 road-circuity factor at about 63 mph. This is an estimate, not a routed drive time, and it counts only ophthalmologists (not optometrists), including hubs in New Mexico where they are the nearest provider. SAHIE is a single-year program, so uninsured is one year newer than the 5-year ACS vintage used elsewhere. Small-population counties (e.g. Loving, ~64 residents) carry wide margins of error, flagged on the county card where relevant.

    Region. 96 counties: the six West Texas Councils of Governments (Panhandle, South Plains, Permian Basin, Concho Valley, West Central Texas, and the Trans-Pecos portion of the Rio Grande COG), plus Cottle County (Texoma COG), included to close a geographic gap. El Paso County is excluded as an urban referral hub with an established ophthalmology workforce. County boundaries: U.S. Census Bureau TIGER (public domain).
